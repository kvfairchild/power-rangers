About the Power Plant Explorer

This site was created to visualize the changing landscape of the U.S. power industry, from specific power plants to major trends.

Cheap shale gas, declining renewable costs, emissions regulations, and new technologies are currently combining to rapidly reshape the power plant landscape in ways that have historically taken decades for this capital-intensive industry.

The complex and dynamic nature of the U.S. energy industry makes accurate and up-to-date data difficult to obtain in an easily consumable format. Data is sparsely distributed across government and industry sponsored websites, often in the form of massive, dense spreadsheets.

The Power Plant Explorer was developed by Joe Hooker, Katherine Gallagher, and James Gearheart using readily available data from the Energy Information Administration (EIA) and Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). This tool is primarily an interactive map that allows a user to visualize the changes occurring in the industry. Each power plant is mapped to a circle that is sized based a selected metric (capacity, generation, CO2 emissions) and colored based on the plant fuel type (e.g. natural gas, coal, wind, etc.). You are able to filter by various criteria and can select a particular power plant to learn more about it.

All filters are dynamically linked so that all visual elements are automatically synced. For example, if you select Massachusetts from the filter, then the map, bar chart, and area chart are all restricted to Massachusetts data.

Example Analysis

Fuel types by year built

One of the most immediately evident patterns is the change in fuel types based on the year that the plant was built.

To perform this analysis, select a range based on the “Year Built” filter and drag that range across the available time frame.


This analysis shows that in the early 1900s, the primary power for the U.S. was hydro and wood. The oil and gas fuel types began to appear early in the 20th century while the coal sector grew rapidly in the middle of the 20th century. The 1960s - 1970s began to see the emergence of nuclear power and by the 21st century, the newly constructed power plants were primarily gas. The current investments in power plant construction are dominated by renewable energy sources such as wind and and emerging solar market.

Capacity and location

Our next analysis focused on the distribution of power plant based on energy capacity. By selecting a range of power capacity and dragging it across the available time period, the user can see the distribution of plant size across the U.S.


This analysis shows that nearly all states have a numerous smaller capacity power plants to meet regional power consumption demands. As the power capacity limits are increased, the number of power plants decrease and their location is centered on the more populated regions. The largest power plants are located near major cities.

CO2 emissions over time

Another type of analysis that can be performed is to look at the level of CO2 emissions over time. In this analysis, we begin by selecting the CO2 emissions metric from the “Size by” filter and selecting a beginning range from the “Year Built” slider.


By increasing the range of the Year Built slider, we can see how CO2 emissions have increased as new power plants have been built and we can see that coal plants have the largest impact on CO2 emissions.

Further Analysis

Here are some ideas for further questions you can use the Power Plant Explorer to answer:

  • What types of power plants are located in the U.S. (e.g. nuclear, solar, etc…), how big are they, and where are they located?
  • Which types of power plants are responsible for the most CO2 emissions?
  • What is the historic trend for energy production? Are renewables growing? Are coal plants retiring?
  • What is the power plant mix for a particular state?
  • How has a power plant’s generation changed over time?

Our hope is that the Power Plant Explorer makes U.S. power industry data accessible for everyone to easily discover this vital part of our society.
