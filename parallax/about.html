About the Power Plant Explorer

This site was created to visualize the changing landscape of the U.S. power industry, from specific power plants to major trends.

Cheap shale gas, declining renewable costs, emissions regulations, and new technologies are currently combining to rapidly reshape the power plant landscape in ways that have historically taken decades for this capital-intensive industry.

The complex and dynamic nature of the U.S. energy industry makes accurate and up-to-date data difficult to obtain in an easily consumable format. Data is sparsely distributed across government- and industry-sponsored websites, often in the form of massive, dense spreadsheets.

The Power Plant Explorer was developed by Joe Hooker, Katherine Gallagher, and James Gearheart using public data from the Energy Information Administration (EIA) and the Environmental Protection Agency (EPA). This tool is primarily an interactive map that allows a user to visualize the changes occurring in the industry. Each power plant is mapped to a circle that is sized based on a selected metric (capacity, generation, or CO2 emissions) and colored based on the power plant type (e.g. natural gas, coal, wind, etc.). A user is able to filter by various criteria and can select a particular power plant to learn more about it.

All filters are dynamically linked so that all visual elements are automatically synced. For example, if you select Massachusetts from the filter, then the map, bar chart, and area chart are all restricted to Massachusetts data.

Example Analysis

Fuel types by year built

One of the most immediately evident patterns is the change in fuel types based on the year that the plant was built.

To perform this analysis, select a range based on the “Year Built” filter and drag that range across the available time frame.


This analysis shows that the oldest plants that are still operating today are primarily hydroelectric plants. In fact, the largest plant in the entire US, the Grand Coulee dam, was built in 1941 and provides more than 7 GW of hydroelectric capacity. In the middle of the twentieth century, many coal plants were built and in the last quarter of the century is when almost all of the nuclear plants were built. Since 2000, gas has been the dominant power plant type to be built, and in the last decade, renewables such as wind and solar have grown rapidly in concert with gas capacity.

Capacity and location

Our next analysis focuses on the distribution of power plants based on energy capacity. By selecting a range of power capacity and dragging it across the available time period, the user can see the distribution of plant sizes across the U.S.


This analysis reveals a few trends. First, the nuclear plants are generally very large. This makes sense because there are significant economies to scale to building nuclear plants, such as permitting, safety measures, and operations centers. Second, Florida has several very large natural gas plants. Third, the largest power plants are generally located right next to major cities.

CO2 emissions over time

Another type of analysis that can be performed is to look at the level of CO2 emissions over time. In this analysis, we begin by selecting the CO2 emissions metric from the “Size by” filter and selecting a beginning range from the “Year Built” slider.


By increasing the range of the Year Built slider, we can see that most of the CO2 emissions come from old coal plants. Natural gas plant capacity has increase significantly after 2000, but the CO2 emissions from gas plants are significantly less than that of coal plants. However, one must also consider the methane emissions that are emitted due to upstream processes in the natural gas industry to understand its total impact on climate change.

Further Analysis

Here are some additional questions a user can answer with the Power Plant Explorer:

  • What types of power plants are located in the U.S. (e.g. nuclear, solar, etc…), how big are they, and where are they located?
  • Which types of power plants don't emit CO2?
  • What is the historic trend for energy production? Are renewables growing? Are coal plants retiring?
  • What is the power plant mix for a particular state?
  • How has a power plant’s generation changed over time?

Our hope is that the Power Plant Explorer makes U.S. power industry data accessible for everyone. After all, electricity is one of the most vital components of our modern society.
